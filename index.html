What is Daylight Saving?

The practice of turning clocks ahead one hour on the second Sunday in March to better match daylight with people's daily schedules by giving more light in the evenings. Back one hour on the first Sunday in November to return back to standard time.


But this twice-yearly disruption comes at a real cost to our health, safety, and daily lives.

The difference in light isn't just about convenience.

Shifting the clock has real physiological consequences, starting with how we sleep.

When the clock springs forward, our bodies don't automatically adjust. Morning sunlight, the most powerful cue for our internal clock, arrives an hour later, throwing off our entire circadian rhythm.

The Circadian Rhythm Disruption

When clocks shift abruptly, the body needs time to readjust, affecting sleep quality, mood, and alertness. The shift disrupts sleep schedules, lowering sleep efficiency, making the case for a stable, permanent time.

As it raises the concerns of about what happens on our roads when we don't get enough sleep and visibility.

Lost sleep affects our body.

When people are sleep deprived and driving in the dark, the consequences show up on the road.

The combination of driver fatigue from the spring forward and reduced visibility from early winter sunsets creates a dangerous situation.

Safety Concerns

Losing one simple hour of sleep has consequences beyond tiredness. Disrupted sleep from clock changes, and reduced visibility during winter standard time increases safety concerns.

Driver Fatigue

Clock changes disrupt the body's natural rhythm, leading to driver fatigue in the days following the transition, increasing the risk of serious collisions.

Reduced Visibility

Under standard time, sunset falls before 4:30 PM in winter, meaning commuters are frequently driving home in complete darkness, raising collision risk.

The evidence points to a clear problem.
